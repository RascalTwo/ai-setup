The problem

Ask an AI whether your plan is good and it will tell you your plan is good. That agreement is cheapest exactly when the stakes are highest — you've already decided, you're invested in the conclusion, and now the one thing in the room that could talk you out of it is nodding along. That's not a flaw in you; it's how minds work. It's just useless.

The turn

Steelman flips the role. The AI is the advocate; you are the judge. It builds the case for the way its most capable proponent would, then the case against at equal strength — the best objection, the failure mode, the thing you're motivated not to see. No strawmen. Then it names the cruxes: the load-bearing assumptions that, if flipped, flip the answer.

It resolves every fact it can itself — codebase, web, memory — and only taps you where your private knowledge, risk tolerance, or values are the deciding factor. One crux at a time, not a barrage. Then it stops and hands you the verdict. It may offer a labeled tentative lean; it never substitutes that for your call.

When the opposition feels soft: one mind arguing with itself can produce a polite, symmetric both-sides where nothing lands. On calls that matter, it escalates — spawning an independent subagent whose only mandate is to kill the idea. A prosecutor with no obligation to be fair. Its independence is the whole value.

When to fire it

Hard-to-reverse or high-stakes calls:

  • An architectural pattern you'll live inside for a year.
  • A dependency choice.
  • A delete or migrate — the things you can't take back.
  • Picking a direction over expensive alternatives.

Works in both directions: before a decision it's a pre-mortem (catch it before you pay for it); after something's built it's a red-team. Skip trivial and reversible calls — proportionality. For small ones, the in-context pass is the right amount of effort.

How it's wired in

A steelman won't self-invoke — which is exactly why it's a standing rule rather than a skill you're trusted to remember. AGENTS.md §3 (Planning & Verification) makes it mandatory before any hard-to-reverse or high-stakes decision.
